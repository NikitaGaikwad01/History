True Story of Internet

The concept of data communication – transmitting data through an electromagnetic medium such as radio or an electric wire – pre-dates the introduction of the first computers. Semaphore lines, telegraph systems and telex machines can be considered early precursors of this kind of communication. Information theory, as enunciated by Claude Shannon, in the 1940s, provided a firm theoretical underpinning to understand the trade-offs between signal-to-noise ratio, bandwidth, and error-free transmission in the presence of noise. The precursor to the Internet was jump started in the early days of computing history. In 1969, ARPA-funded researchers developed many of the protocols used today. The backbone for the ubiquitous stream of emails, media, Facebook postings and tweets that are now shared online every day.

 

History

Vinton Cerf and Bob Kahn are credited with inventing the Internet protocols we use today. The World Wide Web is the brain behind what we think of as the Internet. It is an intricate set of protocols and rules that someone had to develop before we could access it. Long-distance networking between computers was first accomplished in 1969. Researchers at UCLA and Stanford created the first two-node network. The experiment was the first test of "packet switching," a method of transferring data between two computers. Packet switching separates information into smaller "packets" of data. Cerf and Kahn's TCP/IP provided the backbone for an efficient web of interconnected networks. The TCP part of the protocol is in charge of packing the data before it moves across the network. The IP component acts as the trip coordinator and maps the movement of information. The history of the Internet has its origin in efforts to build and interconnect computer networks that arose from research and development in the United States. Computer science was an emerging discipline in the late 1950s that began to consider time-sharing between computer users. ARPANET adopted the packet switching technology proposed by Davies and Baran underpinned by mathematical work in the early 1970s. Since the mid-1990s, the Internet has had a revolutionary impact on culture, commerce, and technology. Research at CERN in Switzerland by British computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee resulted in the World Wide Web. Increasing amounts of data are transmitted at higher and higher speeds over fiber-optic networks. The Internet's takeover of the global communication landscape was rapid in historical terms.
